# Talking Mental

**Mental health plays a significant role in our healing journey.**

By Jolie Lizana

---

Pulmonary hypertension (PH) is more than just a physical condition. The overwhelming thoughts and shattered dreams can be crippling. We have to pick ourselves up and keep moving, but in which direction?

It’s incredibly challenging to reimagine and rebuild your life. It took me nearly 30 years to get it right the first time. “Damned if you do, damned if you don’t” suddenly takes on a whole new meaning!

Learning and liking new things takes time. It’s trial and error all over again in the middle of my life. Unless you’ve experienced this, it’s hard to grasp the gravity of what I’m saying. It’s not fun!

I had an education, a career as a sonographer, and I was a mom. That defined me back then. I had a Type A personality, and everything was done perfectly the first time, tied up in a neat bow. I worked very hard at everything I did.

Now, suddenly, I feel like a loser and a failure. I’m disabled and don’t have an income. I can’t work a 20-hour week without ending up in the hospital, near death. It isn’t perfect!

Don’t get me wrong, I’m grateful I can get out of bed and walk at all! There was a time when I couldn’t walk, get out of bed, or even sit up because I couldn’t breathe. So, I’m very grateful to be alive, to see my son grow up, to watch the sunset, and to swim.

I love the water. When I was on the IV pump, I was miserable because I couldn’t get in the water. Again, I’m grateful. I feel like I should be more… More what? I don’t know.

These thoughts have plagued me for 11 years. I have to remind myself that I have been doing more than most people I know for nearly all of my life. Even then, I always felt like I should be doing more.

Maybe it was the drive. Perhaps now it’s just that I feel ‘less of’ it. I feel like I’m less of the mom I used to be, less of a friend, less of a cook, a cleaner… Always thinking I’m less. That’s always in the back of my mind.

We have to acknowledge the pain and heartache that comes with this diagnosis. We have to drop the heaviness. In theory, it makes perfect sense. In reality, it’s one of the most challenging things I’ve ever had to do.

How am I supposed to accept that my dreams are gone, my hard work went down the drain, and I have no clue who I am or what I want to do with my life, or what my body will even allow me to do?

And in the back of my mind, I’m thinking whatever I do has to be something easy because it won’t be long before my body starts to reach an ‘age’ where I can’t see well or hold my hands steady.

Reconciling with myself and finding a new version of myself is the most challenging part of my life, most days. It’s been so long that you might think I should be used to my life, but I still hope to wake up and feel well enough to go for a run.

I ran 4 miles a day until I couldn’t… I was a runner. That’s something I’m not yet ready to discuss. I pretend it never happened. I can’t lose one more thing. But wow, did running make me feel incredible. It was my meditation.

Losing one’s identity is arguably one of life’s most devastating curses. Giving up things as we age sucks, but it comes on gradually. Well, except the eyesight thing. That’s overnight. What’s up with that? Right?

So, how do I get through this day? Just like everyone else with PH. Timidly, sadly, fearfully; yet fearlessly, humorously, and with moxie.”
