# A Rant About Passing Out and Not Being Vigilant

By Jolie Lizana

---

I've passed out more times than I care to admit—whether it was while doing laundry, lifting too much, or pushing through when I should have listened to my body. The worst experience was at Wrigley Field when they were remodeling the stands, and there were no handrails on the long flight of stairs leading down to the concrete bleacher-style seats. Our seats were located far down. I had set an alarm to go off about the dangers of heading down. I focused on being very careful not to fall. I didn't think about walking back up.

Before the game even started, I needed to use the restroom. On my way up, I had to pause about halfway. Without a handrail, I started to panic, desperately searching for something to hold onto for stability. There was nothing.

I contemplated stepping down into the seats, but the area where I stopped offered no level ground to step sideways. My eyes were darting around, and my heart raced. I decided to go back up. All of this, pausing to catch my breath and deciding to hurry up the last 6 steps, happened within 2 seconds. It was fast. I was about to pass out, but I couldn't get down.

I rushed to the top, where there should have been rails along the walkway. NOPE! By that time, I was so close to passing out that my vision was nearly gone—I could only see faint figures- shadows and streaks of light. Before I was able to get myself down to the ground safely, I passed out.

I lost consciousness in public and ended up with a severe knot on the back of my head. This led to a visit to the ER for a CT scan of my head, and I ended up missing the only game I would have ever seen there. I can’t complain too much, though; I’m lucky to have survived that. I take blood thinners, so there was concern for a brain bleed.

Now, I always pause and ask myself, "Can I do this safely?" Not only do I feel well enough, but what if I start feeling bad? Are there ways in the situation or at the location that would be unsafe for me if I start feeling badly?"

Whether it’s climbing stairs, leaning over, or taking a shower, I’ve learned to listen to my intuition. Fainting isn’t just inconvenient; it’s dangerous. Trusting my body’s signals has become crucial for my well-being.

We need to learn our limits, including where we are safe and where we are not, although it may take time. Be kind to yourself.
