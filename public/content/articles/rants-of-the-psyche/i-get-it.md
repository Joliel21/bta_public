# I Get It!

**Surviving the first couple of years after diagnosis.**

By Jolie Lizana

---

I had to give it everything I had at my disposal to fight, which was medicine. There was nothing other than taking medication and having an IV pump of meds to my heart that would help me. I knew this! I remember thinking that if it’s all I have to fight with, I would have to deal with side effects.

I had to fight and keep going for my son. I had nothing else to fight with. I was physically unable to do any fighting. By the time I was FINALLY diagnosed, I had bilateral pulmonary embolisms, right-sided heart failure, systolic heart failure, and diastolic heart failure.

I spent 3/4 of the first year I was diagnosed in the hospital. I could hardly walk the four-foot distance from my bed to the bathroom, both at the hospital and at home. My furniture was moved downstairs because I couldn’t even go upstairs at this point. All too often, I couldn’t get water from the kitchen. It was too far away. My 5-year-old son brought me water.

I would get a really big cup of water, a pitcher of water, and any bottled waters we had to keep next to me in a basket under my nightstand when I was able to go. My little boy brought me bananas and granola bars as well—just things he could grab easily.

I was titrating so fast that I didn’t eat much of anything for what must have been a year or two. I was just so very sick from the meds. I ate so much more at the hospital because they brought me three meals a day, but I was still unable to eat a full meal.

I had family who stayed at my home to take care of my son and the home when I was in the hospital. I had family members who cleaned, did laundry, cooked, and did so much more when I was in the hospital and at home.

I had family in town and about 6 hours away who were there for me when I really needed them. As difficult as it was to ask for help to do what is so easy for the average person, they educated themselves about my disease. They “get it,” and I am so very blessed.

After I stopped taking that particular medication, I was doing SO much better. I had to wait for other meds to get FDA approval. Those meds changed my life! I began to live again. I have since added new medications to my regimen, and titration was very challenging. Once I hit my maintenance dose on this new medication, life has been easier. I still can’t do as much as I’d like to do! Some days I can do more than others; you know the drill.

We all have this, and it can be overwhelmingly frustrating. We can’t make schedules for ourselves or always keep our plans with family/friends. It’s equally hard on us, as we often feel we’ve let others down… again.

I hope your family and friends take the time to educate themselves about PH. Knowing what to expect and understanding that we cannot control our bodies at any given moment in the day may help them understand it, too.

PHers are all so different, and many people have other health issues, which makes this a difficult topic to help others with. I have other health issues combined with nerve and disk damage in my neck/back. So I get it. You are not alone in your frustration about not being able to clean your home as you used to. We are suddenly unable to keep up with the house, cook, run errands, take care of our children, attend doctors’ appointments, and still have the much-needed downtime we all need.

There were times when I couldn’t do anything, so there wasn’t much cleaning to be done. There were definitely times between titrations when I had the opportunity and a bit of strength to clean, but I did not take them. That was not even on my radar! I was in a fight for my life!

When I was titrating up, I took as few days as possible in between because I was on the losing end of the PH battle. I knew I had to give this disease a serious fight back if I wanted to live. It was that simple. So in those few “well” days between titrating up, when I was able to get out of bed, I ALWAYS chose to spend them with my son.

When you are too sick to get out of bed, too sick to even sit up in the living room, the last thing you are thinking of is cleaning the house. Spending quality time with my son was ALL I wanted to do. I did not know how much more time we had together.

I wasn’t expected to come out of this fight ahead. I gave it my all and still do. So, cleaning is not what people who love you want you to do when you are finally able to get out of bed and into the “land of the living”. After getting on a maintenance dose, I was able to do so much more! But even then, things were not easy.

Before my body began to crash, I was able to keep up with my busy schedule at work, take care of my son, do the shopping, laundry, cook nightly dinners, participate in my son’s school activities, sew the 30+ children’s costumes for plays and performances, bake goodies for every event my son was a part of, planned wonderful birthdays, and holiday parties at our home, and cut 3 acres of land.

I did it all while I drove 5 1/2 hours, 5 days a week. As I continued with this rigorous schedule, my health deteriorated. First to go were the nightly dinners, then the housework, then the shopping… You get the idea. I was getting worse. I missed days of college because I was so sick. I missed days of college because I had doctor’s appointments.

I had to clean our ENTIRE house after contractors fixed the subfloor in our place. They did not close off upstairs, and everything up there was covered in 1/2 of dirt and sawdust. It was horrible! After cleaning out literally every cabinet in the house, I then had to put everything upstairs away and unpack and put away everything that had been boxed up for us; I was still in college at this point. This was difficult, and I felt like I was going to die.

I was extremely sick during the first couple of years. I was too sick to do any cleaning or cooking for a long time. Even when I could, I was at the whim of my diseased body. We often don’t have control over our bodies. Please do not push yourself.

I did push myself and have passed out many times doing things when I should have listened to myself, or better yet, questioned myself. I passed out more than once doing laundry. I’ve passed out carrying heavy objects and generally exhausting myself.

Since the last time I passed out going up the stairs at Wrigley Field, I have really been listening to myself. The stadium was being remodeled. I didn’t know this, but when we went to our seats, an alarm went off. I remember thinking, “They have no handrails!”

I blew it off and went down the stairs carefully. Well, when I went up the stairs, I didn’t think much about the rails until I needed them. I knew I was going to pass out if I didn’t have a rail to hold me for a minute while my heart rate slowed and my breathing was better. NO rail! I didn’t listen to that alarm.

I listen to my intuitions now. I have also become more aware of my physical limitations by pausing to question myself before making a trip up the stairs, bending over, reaching overhead, taking a bath or shower, etc. Many things can cause us to pass out. Please listen to your intuition and your body. Pause, ask yourself how you feel, and how what you do will tax your lungs and/or heart.

Crashing (as I understand it) is hitting rock bottom, being unable to help yourself physically. I couldn’t even sit up to breathe when I was finally diagnosed. I had to get help walking to the SUV that was parked in the grass at the bottom of the porch stairs; then I had to curl up in the front seat, lying on my right side, in the SUV on the way to the ER. I didn’t think I was going to make it as we crossed the bridge alive; bump after bump, I felt like my heart was going to burst.

It was a horrible day in my life!
