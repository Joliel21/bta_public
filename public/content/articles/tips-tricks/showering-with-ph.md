# Showering with PH

**Showering can be exhausting.**

By Jolie Lizana

Publication date: September 16, 2025

---

Safety note: These tips are based on personal experience and are for general support only. Pulmonary hypertension (PH), heart failure, oxygen needs, dizziness, fainting, shortness of breath, IV therapy, central lines, dressing care, infection risk, and shower safety should be discussed with your doctor, PH care team, specialty pharmacy, or infusion team. If showering makes you dizzy, faint, short of breath, or causes chest pain, affects your IV site, or feels unsafe, stop and seek medical guidance.

Here are some tips and tricks.

Steam or water vapor may make breathing feel harder for some people. I generally leave the bathroom door open with the ventilation fan on. Sometimes it helps to leave a small crack in the shower curtain or bathroom door to improve airflow.

Strength, weakness, and fatigue can make showering difficult. Getting in and out of the bath, standing in the shower, washing hair, and drying off can take more energy than expected. I use a teak stool. The wood can handle the water. I have heard of people using a plastic chair.

Long hair can be exhausting to clean. I have cut my hair off after getting out of several showers. It can be so stressful that sometimes it’s necessary to do what you can and move on. I almost cut my hair again last week. I have been letting it grow, and I love it long, but sometimes it gets so tangled when I am stuck in bed sick for a few days. I’ve managed to let it grow out by doing my best to keep it from getting too knotted while I’m tossing and turning in bed, and by not washing it every time I shower. I have purchased a shower cap that has helped me so much. I love that thing. A wet brush is very helpful when used alongside a leave-in conditioner.

IV lines bring on a whole new set of challenges. If you have an IV line, central line, or infusion site, ask your PH care team, specialty pharmacy, or infusion team exactly how they want you to protect it while showering. The IV site and dressing should stay dry and secure unless your care team has given you different instructions.

The pharmacy that provided my IV medication also supplied water-barrier bandages. I was also advised to try Press’n Seal, then tape around the edges with waterproof bandage tape, followed by the dressing provided by the pharmacy. This is what worked for me, but you should follow your own care team’s instructions.

After showering, check your dressing carefully. If it is wet, loose, lifted, contaminated, painful, red, swollen, draining, or otherwise concerning, contact your care team, specialty pharmacy, or infusion team right away. IV line infections can be serious, so take dressing care and showering instructions seriously.

IV pumps must be handled with care and should not get wet unless your device instructions specifically say otherwise. Ask your specialty pharmacy, infusion team, or PH care team for guidance on protecting your pump during bathing or showering.

When I was on IV therapy, I wrapped my pump in a small towel, placed it inside a zip-top bag, folded that inside a plastic grocery bag, and hung it from the top of the shower on a plastic door wreath hanger. That was my personal setup, not universal medical advice. Follow your pump instructions and your infusion team’s guidance.

What helps me most is using a handheld shower head with a long extension hose, keeping everything I need close by, and using a stool even on days when I do not feel extremely weak or tired. Save energy where you can, and ask your care team what shower setup is safest for you.

I have included some products that I use:

## Shower head splitter

This allows you to control the water flow. It can come from the showerhead and/or the handheld shower.

## Grip for the handheld shower head

This keeps it in a place that is easy to reach. It is nice to have it where it hits you while sitting, just as if you were standing. This also prevents us from leaning over to pick it up, which may reduce dizziness for some people.

## Shower head extender hose

No kinks or anything. I’ve had mine for several years now.

## Keeping everything close to you is great.

This is a great way to be safe and save energy. The fewer times you lean, bend, or put your arms above your head, the better. Bathing and hair-washing movements can take a lot of energy and may make breathing feel more difficult for some people.
