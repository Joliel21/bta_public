# Running Errands/Shopping

**Here are some tips and tricks I use to help manage PH symptoms.**

By Jolie Lizana

Publication date: September 16, 2025

---

Safety note: These tips are based on personal experience and are for general support only. Pulmonary hypertension (PH), heart failure, dizziness, fainting, shortness of breath, oxygen needs, anxiety, driving, mobility limits, and activity restrictions should be discussed with your doctor or PH care team. If shopping, driving, lifting, bending, walking, or errands make you dizzy, faint, short of breath, cause chest pain, trigger severe anxiety, or feel unsafe, stop and seek medical guidance.

Not everyone can leave the house to shop or run errands. I genuinely hope the new shopping and delivery methods are helpful to you. Furthermore, I hope you have family and friends available to support you. Read a bit of my experiences, or scroll on down for the tips & tricks.

Leaving the house can be stressful. When I was first able to leave the house, it took me quite a bit of time to check, double-check, and check again once I was in the car. I had to drive 30-45 mins. to get to a store for groceries and household items. I was worried that something would go wrong with my pump. So I took my backup pump and everything I needed to make a new cartridge with me. When I switched to oral and inhaled therapy, I triple-checked again to ensure I had everything. Leaving the house can cause raised anxiety for so many reasons.

I had to use the basket-equipped scooters to shop for a long time. I was not able to walk far. I didn’t look sick, so I received countless dirty looks and head shakes; I even got those piercing, dagger-like eyes of hate and disgust. It was so upsetting! I wanted to cry at times. I did a bit in the stores but pulled myself back together. I had to take a deep breath, remind myself that I don’t look sick, and that they didn’t know any better. I would often get the groceries into the car, settle into my seat, and break down crying. There were far too many times when I arrived at a store only to find five scooters unplugged and completely drained. I had to sit on the bench and wait for one of them. All too often, I had to stop mid-shopping and drive to a plug-in to recharge.

I was humiliated. I just put my head down and messed with my phone. If my little one were with me, I would watch him play. Sadly, on more than one occasion, I drove 30+minutes to the store and couldn’t get out of the car. I had anxiety attacks. I cried in the parking lot, tried to pull myself together, but I couldn’t. I had to drive straight back home, where I cried more. This disease takes a physical and mental toll on us. Listen to your body and mind.

Things were different when I had my son with me. I sat in the car a couple of times and left, but I had to keep it together. I told my little one that I had changed my mind and we would do our other errands if we had any. If we didn’t, I would take him to the park. I couldn’t stay long at first, but it was great to watch him play.

My son was about five years old when I started leaving the house with him. When he was shopping with me, I had to pretend nothing was happening around me. I had to work intensely and mindfully to block out the looks and the words and focus on my son. He was too little to walk by himself, and I couldn’t get up and catch him if he decided to take off running. At first, he sat on my lap. That was a challenge, so he started standing up and holding on to my legs or the handles. Soon, he asked to steer. I let him drive as I held on to the edge of the handles. He had fun, and that grounded me a bit. I was miserable in that cart, so when my son was having fun, I felt better. I focused on him; it put me in a better mental state.

However, to this day, I get anxious when I go to the grocery store alone. Fortunately, I don’t have to do that often. My son, now 13, comes with me and pushes the buggy for us. We usually go once, maybe twice a month. I get everything I can on our list. The buggy is so full that we use a new buggy to put the bagged groceries in when we check out. If we need anything else during the week, my boyfriend picks it up for us, or I can make a quick trip. If I have to buy anything on my own, it won’t be much. It takes a great deal of energy to push a heavy buggy. Some days are better than others. I can occasionally get groceries by myself, but it uses up all of my energy for a while.

My family doesn’t want that to happen, and they want to help me. And surely, I can find better things to do with my energy, for sure. I never know when I’ll suddenly hit a wall. So I try to get very few things when I go alone. Sadly, we often have other ailments to contend with that we need to factor into how we care for our bodies. I have cervical, thoracic, and sacrum disks that are herniated. I have other ailments, but these are generally the ones that bother me when I’m shopping. I’m lucky to have such a wonderful son to help me and a great boyfriend who never minds popping into the store for us.

If you have someone to accompany you, go when they can and get as much as you can in that one trip. See if you can get help putting the groceries away as well. Take the support offered to you. Use these opportunities to give yourself rest, save energy for something else, or allow your loved ones to help in a way that suits them. PH is not something others can carry for you, but support can still make a meaningful difference. It’s meaningful to your loved ones to assist you. My sister once told me that she needed to know she was making a difference in some way. She helped in more ways than she will ever know.

If you go alone, only get things that are easy to push around the store, load into your car, and unload. Try to take it easy on yourself. Listen to your body and consider the energy you will need when you leave the store.

Deliveries are good, but the cost can add up. If you do order deliveries, remind yourself that you will need the energy to bring the bags inside and unpack anything that needs to be cooled or frozen right away. When delivery is free or affordable, it’s a good idea to calculate your energy based on delivery costs. I value grocery deliveries at times, as they allow me the energy to unpack and cook a meal afterward. If I shop, that’s all I’ve got for the day (most days, anyway).

When you have a lot of groceries, divide them. Place the fridge and freezer items at the top first, so you can take a break afterward if needed. Pace yourself.

When putting away groceries, try using bags to lower any items you have to place in lower cabinets. Get everything low and then sit on a stool or the ground; sit and stock your lower items. Remember not to swing from side to side when getting groceries and turning back to put them away. Repeated twisting, bending, lifting, or reaching may make breathing feel harder for some people. Also, be very careful when reaching up or down; some people may feel dizzy if they do this too quickly or too often in a row.

Get a sturdy, fold-up grocery cart to help you transport items from your vehicle to the kitchen. Check the weight limit on the cart before your purchase.

If you take a few trips a month, try making a list of must-haves and meal essentials until your next visit. Also, try adding an extra day or so to your menu just in case you can’t get there when you anticipated.
