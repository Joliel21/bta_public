# Lost in this Space

**Being alive, but not living, will never be easy.**

By Jolie Lizana

---

Having to give up who you have been for thirty-something years is not easy. I still don’t cope as well as I’d like to. You might think, after 10 years, that I’d move on, but I struggle with this on an all too regular basis.

I was a runner, and then I wasn’t. I was healthy. I ran 4+ miles a day. I had a career, and then I didn’t. I was a sonographer. I had “the” job I’d wanted for a very long time. I loved the ultrasound. It has everything to enjoy. I like helping others, physics, science, evolving technologies, and continued education. I have been disabled for too long.

I hate not working. I can’t work a 20-hour week regularly. My health is too unreliable, and I have too many doctor's appointments. There is nowhere to work that would allow me to take off when I need to recover from activities or for my many doctor’s appointments. It sucks.

For the most part, I lost my life. I’m very fortunate to be alive after all I went through. I crashed pretty hard. When the PH was diagnosed, I had bilateral PEs, 3 types of heart failure, and, well, obviously very high PH. My heart’s ejection fraction was quite low, my BMP was over 400, and I had to lie down and breathe. I am grateful to be here.

Sometimes, when I get sad because I can’t work or run, I remind myself of when I couldn’t walk. It helps me put things in perspective, but only for a moment. Soon, I began to think about the “Why?” of it all. I know I will never have an answer for this, but I still wonder.

I started walking and running in my late 20s. I started because I knew something was wrong with my lungs. I was always athletic. My muscles would not hurt. Nowhere hurt. I would run out of breath too easily. It didn’t make sense to me.

One day, my gym teacher told me I had bends. He said it was not something that could be fixed. He said, “You’re fine.” As a child, I foolishly thought that adults knew the answers. I thought my PE teacher would understand because it has to do with health.

No doctor gave me any reason. These doctors never saw what happened when I ran and how it made me feel. At one point, I decided that I wanted to have a family and share healthy habits with my child. I thought running would be a wonderful habit to pass along. And I could improve my lungs while I ran. So I started.

It took me a long time to build up my lung endurance. I finally could run a mile wide open without pause. It was amazing! I believe that had I not started running, I would not have survived the crash that occurred years later. I try to tell myself that I was not meant to be a runner. I ran to save my life for what was to come. It somehow makes me feel better.

Losing myself in so many ways is just hard, especially because I worked so hard to get there, but we either pick ourselves up and move forward or sit in our pity. I want to move on! It’s a conscious choice, but it takes effort.

New opportunities will present themselves if we open our eyes and minds a bit. It just takes time, but all good things do!
